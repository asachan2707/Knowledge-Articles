Claude Has Changed My Approach to Learning New Things

After three years with AI learning tools, the shift from passive consumption to active participation changed everything. How to use Claude as a genuine learning partner — not just a search engine with better grammar.

SourceFrank Andrade — Artificial Corner, May 2026
Tools comparedClaude Cowork, ChatGPT, NotebookLM

The problem with AI-assisted learning is not access to information — it is the same problem as Google: you can find anything but retain almost nothing. Reading AI answers is still passive consumption. The shift that changes retention is making the AI question you, not just answer you.

Core idea: Active recall — being tested, forced to explain, asked to apply — beats re-reading or watching by an order of magnitude. Claude is the most flexible tool available for building active recall sessions around any topic.

Why three tools instead of one

After testing everything, the honest answer is that different AI tools have different strengths for learning. ChatGPT is best for initial topic exploration — broad, fast, good at mapping the conceptual landscape. NotebookLM is best when you have specific materials: upload your own PDFs, lecture notes, or articles and it grounds every answer to those sources. Claude is best for the learning session itself — the interactive practice, the Socratic questioning, the explanation-then-correction loop that builds real understanding.

The Claude learning techniques

1. The Feynman technique, automated

The Feynman technique: explain a concept as if teaching it to someone who knows nothing about it. Gaps in your explanation reveal gaps in your understanding. Claude can run this automatically: 

I just read about [topic]. I'm going to explain it to you
as if you know nothing. After I finish, tell me:
1. What I got right
2. What I got wrong or oversimplified
3. What I left out that matters

This is more useful than asking Claude to explain the topic to you, because the act of generating the explanation is where the learning happens — not receiving it.

2. Socratic questioning mode

I want to understand [topic] deeply, not just superficially.
Don't explain it to me. Instead, ask me a sequence of questions
that will lead me to understand it myself. Start simple.
Increase difficulty only when I demonstrate I've grasped the prior step.

This is the closest thing to a one-on-one tutor most people will ever have access to. Claude adapts the difficulty in real time based on your answers, asks follow-up questions when your answer is incomplete, and redirects when you are on the wrong track.

3. Build a custom learning profile

Tell Claude your background once at the start of a session and it adjusts every explanation to your level: 

My background: [your relevant experience].
My goal: understand [topic] well enough to [specific outcome].
My current understanding: [what you already know].
Gaps: [what you're specifically uncertain about].

Now teach me [topic] in a way that's calibrated to this profile.

The profile does more than adjust vocabulary. It changes the analogies Claude uses, the depth it goes to on each sub-concept, and which prerequisites it skips vs. reviews.

4. Spaced repetition through conversation

At the end of a learning session, ask Claude to generate a review set: 

Based on everything we covered in this session, give me:
1. 5 flashcard-style Q&As (question on one side, answer on other)
2. 3 application scenarios where I'd need to use this knowledge
3. The single most important thing to remember a month from now

Save these. Return to the application scenarios in a week without looking at the answers first. That single act of retrieval practice does more for long-term retention than re-reading the session transcript.

5. The gap-finding interview

I think I understand [topic]. Interview me about it.
Ask probing questions designed to find where my understanding
breaks down. Don't go easy — I want to find the gaps.

This is the most efficient way to identify what to study next. Five minutes of this is worth an hour of re-reading, because it targets exactly what you do not know rather than covering ground you already covered.

Voice vs text

Why voice dictation often beats voice mode

Claude's voice mode is convenient but loses something important: the friction of typing forces you to articulate your thinking more precisely. Voice dictation (speaking → auto-transcribed text in the input field) gives you the speed of speaking while keeping the precision of text. For learning sessions specifically, that precision matters — vague questions get vague answers.

Key Takeaway

Use ChatGPT to map a topic, NotebookLM to work with your own materials, and Claude to practice active recall. The mistake is using Claude the same way you use a search engine — asking it to explain things at you. The value is in the other direction: letting Claude question you, identify your gaps, and force you to generate explanations rather than receive them.
