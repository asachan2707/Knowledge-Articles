This Very Subtle Yet Common Sign Screams "AI Wrote This"

The patterns that trained readers recognise immediately as AI-generated — and the specific techniques to eliminate them so your writing sounds like you, not like a language model that has read the entire internet.

Portal Internal · Curated
Focus AI writing tell-signs, voice authenticity, editing for human tone
Published May 2026

The problem is not that AI writes badly. The problem is that AI writes in a recognisable register — a particular blend of confident hedging, structural predictability, and vocabulary that signals "this was generated, not thought." Editors, hiring managers, and experienced readers notice it in the first two sentences without being able to name it. This piece names it.

Core idea: AI-generated text is recognisable not because it is wrong but because it is statistically average. Every word choice, transition, and sentence structure tends toward the most common pattern in the training data. Human writing is full of deliberate deviations. That's the difference.

Story · "I could tell immediately"

The consistent report from experienced readers is not "this contains factual errors" — it is "something felt off after the first paragraph." That feeling is pattern recognition: the text is too clean, too structured, too comprehensive, and entirely free of the authorial decisions that make human writing distinctive.

The tell-signs

What trained readers spot immediately

1. The list obsession

AI defaults to bullet points and numbered lists for almost every multi-part idea. Human writers use lists selectively — when parallelism genuinely serves the content. When you see a piece that has broken every paragraph into three bullets, that structure was chosen by a probability distribution, not a writer.

2. The opening acknowledgement

AI frequently opens by restating the question or acknowledging the topic: "This is a great question," "When it comes to X, there are many important considerations," "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..." These openers are a statistical signature — the model has learned that essays begin with a thesis setup, and it generates the most common version of one.

3. Hedging without specificity

"It's important to note that..." / "It's worth mentioning that..." / "While this can vary..." These phrases add qualification without adding information. Human writers who hedge do so with a specific reason: "In cold climates, this won't apply because..." AI hedges in the abstract because it has learned that hedged writing is statistically safer.

4. Transition word overuse

"Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "In conclusion," "It is crucial to..." These are the connective tissue of formal writing — and AI has learned to use them constantly. Human writers use them sparingly or replace them with sentence-level logic that makes the transition implicit.

5. The comprehensive coverage reflex

AI answers feel complete in a way that human answers rarely do. Ask a human expert about a topic and they will tell you what they know well and acknowledge what they don't. AI covers every angle at approximately equal depth, which reads as suspiciously thorough — like a Wikipedia summary, not a person with a perspective.

6. Perfect paragraph symmetry

Sections of uniform length, bullet points with similar word counts, conclusions that mirror introductions — human writing has natural irregularity because ideas don't divide evenly. When everything is the same length, a machine chose the structure.

7. No opinion, only synthesis

The subtlest signal: AI describes what is without ever saying what it thinks. "Some argue X, others argue Y, and the best approach depends on your context." Human writers have positions — even tentative, qualified, provisional ones. A piece with no discernible stance on anything it discusses is statistically AI.

How to write past the tells

Practical editing techniques

  • Add one sentence that could only come from you. A specific memory, a counterintuitive position, an observation from your particular context. Even one such sentence anchors the writing to a real person.
  • Break the list. Find the most important bullet and turn it into a paragraph. Add a concrete example. The others can stay as bullets or become sentence fragments — but the promoted one should read like a human chose to dwell there.
  • Cut every hedging opener. Delete every sentence that starts with "It's important to note," "It's worth mentioning," or "In today's world." Replace the content without the frame, or delete the sentence entirely — the paragraph almost always reads better.
  • Change one transition to a hard cut. Instead of "Furthermore, this approach has..." try "This has a second effect:" or just a new paragraph with no connector. Hard cuts feel like human decisions.
  • State your actual position. Find the most debatable claim in the piece and say what you think about it without hedging. "I think X is the right call here because..." is human writing. "Both approaches have merit" is AI writing.
  • Read it aloud. AI writing has a particular rhythm — consistent, measured, slightly formal. Human speech has irregular rhythm. If it sounds like a corporate voiceover, it sounds like AI. Edit until a sentence forces you to pause naturally, stumble slightly, or react.
Common questions

FAQ

  • Does this mean I shouldn't use AI for writing at all?

    No. Use AI for drafts, structure, and research synthesis. The mistake is publishing the first draft. Every professional writer edits; the AI-tells problem is an editing problem, not a generation problem. A heavily edited AI draft sounds human; an unedited one doesn't.

  • Are AI detection tools reliable?

    Not reliably. They have high false positive rates and are easily fooled by minor editing. The human test — "does this read like a person wrote it?" — is still more accurate than any detector. Train your own eye rather than relying on tools.

  • Is this problem getting better as models improve?

    Partially. Newer models produce less formulaic output and can mimic specific voices better with few-shot examples. But the fundamental issue — that the model optimises for statistical averages — doesn't disappear with scale. The tells shift; they don't vanish.

Key Takeaway

AI writing is recognisable because it is statistically average — comprehensive, hedged, structured, and free of actual positions. The fix is not better prompting; it is editing. Cut the hedges, break the symmetry, add one sentence only you could write, and state what you think. That is all it takes to stop sounding like a language model.
