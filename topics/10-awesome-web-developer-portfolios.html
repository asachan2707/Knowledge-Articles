10 Awesome Web Developer Portfolios

Ten real developer portfolios worth studying before you build your own — plus the structural questions you need to answer first and the patterns that separate memorable portfolios from forgettable ones.

Portal CodeBurst · Medium
Focus Portfolio inspiration and design decisions for developers
Original source codeburst.io

Your portfolio is the only piece of work where you have complete creative control. It is a live product demo and a first impression simultaneously. Most developers treat it as an afterthought — a checkbox before job applications. The portfolios worth studying treat it as a design exercise with real stakes.

Core idea: Study strong portfolios before building your own. The design decisions that look obvious in hindsight — page count, what to lead with, how to show technical credibility — are rarely obvious upfront.

Story · The question nobody answers first

The framing question the article opens with is deceptively simple: "Should it be simple or complex? Single page or multiple? What should you showcase first?" Most developers skip straight to picking a framework and a color scheme. The portfolios worth referencing all answered the structural questions first, then built.

Patterns across all ten

What the best ones have in common

  • Personal domain or a credible subdomain: Every portfolio uses either a branded personal domain or GitHub Pages. No free website builders with their branding in the URL.
  • Dual-skill positioning is a differentiator: Several developers lead with both design and engineering. In a market of pure specialists, this combination stands out.
  • The hosting choice is itself a signal: GitHub Pages tells a hiring manager that the developer owns their deployment pipeline. It is a subtle but real data point.
  • Credibility through output, not claims: The strongest portfolios show published books, open-source projects, or shipped products — not bullet-point lists of skills.
  • Structure matches content volume: Single-page portfolios work for focused specialists. Multi-section works when there is genuinely more to show. Complexity for its own sake hurts more than it helps.
Building your own

The structural questions to answer before writing a line of code

  • Who is the primary audience? Hiring managers scan; clients browse; collaborators dig. Your structure should match the audience's behavior.
  • What is your single strongest signal of credibility? Published work, open-source contributions, shipped products, a unique combination of skills. Lead with that.
  • Single page or multi-page? Single page if you have fewer than four distinct sections. Multi-page if the content genuinely needs room.
  • What does the URL say before anyone reads a word? firstname.com > github.io/username > random-subdomain.webflow.io.
  • Can you show the work, not just describe it? Screenshots, live demos, and case studies beat "I built a full-stack app with React and Node" every time.
Common questions

FAQ

  • Should I build it from scratch or use a template?

    If you are applying as a frontend or full-stack developer, building from scratch demonstrates the skills you are claiming. For backend developers or designers, a well-customised template is entirely acceptable — the work itself matters more than how the container was made.

  • How many projects should I include?

    Three to five strong projects beats ten mediocre ones. Hiring managers spend under two minutes on a portfolio. Include only work you can speak to deeply and that shows your range or your depth — not both simultaneously.

  • Is GitHub Pages too "junior"?

    No. Several of the featured portfolios use GitHub Pages. What reads as junior is poor design, thin content, or a generic template — not the hosting provider.

Key Takeaway

The portfolios worth studying answer structural questions before visual ones — who it is for, what leads, how much content it holds. Build from a decision, not from a template.
