Why You Should Read More Books

A reminder that books out-compete algorithms on depth. Long-form reading rebuilds the focus, vocabulary, and mental models that short-form feeds slowly erode.

Focus Reading depth vs algorithmic attention loss

The author opens with a confession most of us share — 45 minutes of bedtime scrolling that produced nothing useful, while a book sitting nearby would have. The article frames reading as an anti-algorithm habit: a deliberate choice to spend attention on dense, structured human thought instead of fragmented entertainment.

Core idea: Books let you borrow years of structured thinking from experts; feeds give you minutes of engineered distraction.

Story · The 45-minute scroll versus one chapter

The piece begins with the writer scrolling through 45 minutes of celebrity drama and dog clips before bed. Entertaining, sure — but a week later, nothing of it remained in memory.

A book in the same 45 minutes would have left an argument, a mental model, or a vivid image. That asymmetry — fleeting amusement vs durable thinking — is the core of the article.

Detailed breakdown

Key insights and why they matter

1) Books are compressed human expertise

A good book is often a person's decade of effort compressed into a few hundred pages. Reading it is one of the cheapest ways to inherit that thinking.

2) Feeds train fragmented attention

Algorithms reward novelty and emotion, not depth. Over time, this rewires you toward shorter attention spans and shallower analysis.

3) Reading improves output, not just input

People who read regularly tend to write, speak, and argue with more nuance — they have absorbed structured arguments and good prose.

4) Choosing books is attention ownership

Every book you pick is a small act of refusing the algorithm's suggestion. That choice itself is a thinking habit.

Practical learning

How to apply this in real work

  • Block 20–30 minutes of pre-sleep reading before opening any feed.
  • Keep two books in parallel: one technical/practical, one narrative/historical.
  • Capture one core idea per chapter in your own words.
  • After finishing, write a one-page summary you could explain to a friend.
Common questions

FAQ

  • Do audiobooks count?

    Yes for retention of ideas; less so for slow, reflective thinking. Mix both based on context.

  • I read but forget everything — what now?

    Switch from passive reading to writing summaries. Retention follows production, not consumption.

  • How many books per year is "enough"?

    Aim for one applied book per month. Depth and application beat raw count.
