Every time you open Uber and tap "Request", the app has found you a nearby driver, computed their ETA accounting for live traffic, priced your fare with a real-time supply-demand ratio, and sent you both a confirmation — in under two seconds. Behind that moment is a system that handles 100 million riders, 5 million drivers, and 1.25 million location writes per second globally. It is not magic; it is a sequence of smart engineering decisions.

Core idea: The speed comes from three layered optimisations — spatial filtering that cuts 5 million drivers down to 50, permanent push connections that eliminate polling, and parallel ETA computation that scores each candidate simultaneously rather than sequentially.

Why this question matters

This problem sits at the intersection of real-time systems, distributed state, and geospatial algorithms — exactly the combination that separates senior engineers from the rest. It has been asked in virtually every FAANG-level system design loop since 2021. Answering it well requires knowing not just the components, but which tools to pick and why you would reject the alternatives.

Your interviewer already knows the happy-path diagram. What they are probing is whether you surface failure modes, quantify the bottlenecks, and design the failure-recovery logic without being prompted.

Deep Dive 1

Geospatial Indexing — Why Uber Uses H3 Hexagons

The naive approach and why it fails

A straight SELECT * FROM drivers WHERE distance(lat, lng, :rider_lat, :rider_lng) < 5000 requires a full table scan of 5 million rows and runs the Haversine formula on every row. Even with a PostGIS R-tree index it cannot sustain 400 ride requests/sec at p99 < 200 ms. You need a lookup structure, not a search.

Geohash (legacy — do not use for production)

  • Interleaves latitude and longitude bits into a base-32 string.
  • Nearby strings share a common prefix — but only approximately. Two points 10 m apart can have completely different hashes if they straddle a grid boundary.
  • Cell shapes are rectangular, so neighbours are not equidistant from the centre.
  • Longitude precision varies: 111 km per degree at the equator, 0 km at the poles.
  • Red flag in interviews: proposing Geohash without mentioning its boundary discontinuity problem signals you have not thought this through.

S2 Geometry (Google's choice)

  • Projects the Earth onto a cube face, then applies a Hilbert space-filling curve.
  • Handles poles naturally; great for coverage algorithms and exact region queries.
  • Computationally heavier and harder to reason about than H3.
  • Best fit: static geofencing, map tile coverage. Overkill for real-time driver lookup.

H3 — Uber's production choice

H3 divides the Earth into a hierarchy of regular hexagons at 16 resolution levels. Hexagons have one key property that rectangles and triangles lack: every neighbour of a hexagon shares a full edge and is equidistant from the centre. That makes radius expansion (searching progressively larger rings of cells) both fast and spatially uniform. 


H3 Resolution  |  Avg cell area   |  Practical use
---------------|------------------|-------------------------------
Res 4          |  1,770 km²       |  Country-level surge zones
Res 7          |  5.16 km²        |  City neighbourhood supply
Res 10         |  0.0296 km²      |  Driver-level matching (~170 m)
Res 12         |  0.0007 km²      |  Precise pickup pin

H3 resolution levels — Uber uses res 10 for driver lookup

How the lookup works

  • Driver comes online: GPS coordinates → H3 cell index at res 10 (e.g. 8a2a1072b59ffff) in O(1). Stored in Redis as a sorted set keyed by cell ID.
  • Rider requests: rider's GPS → H3 cell index. Query Redis for drivers in that cell + its 6 immediate neighbours (ring 1).
  • No drivers in ring 1: expand to ring 2 (18 additional cells), then ring 3, up to ring 5 (~max 5 km at res 10). This is the progressive radius expansion — O(1) per ring because neighbour lookup is a constant-time bitwise operation.
  • Result: instead of scanning 5 million global drivers, you examine 50–100 drivers in adjacent cells. That is the source of the speed.

// Node.js — H3 lookup (h3-js library)
import * as h3 from 'h3-js';

const RESOLUTION = 10;

// Convert GPS to H3 cell
function getH3Cell(lat, lng) {
  return h3.latLngToCell(lat, lng, RESOLUTION);
}

// Get all cells within k rings
function getSearchCells(lat, lng, rings = 1) {
  const center = getH3Cell(lat, lng);
  return h3.gridDisk(center, rings); // returns center + all cells in k rings
}

// Example: rider at (40.7128, -74.0060), search ring 1
const cells = getSearchCells(40.7128, -74.0060, 1);
// → 7 cells (center + 6 neighbours)
// Use these cell IDs to query Redis

WebSocket Location Pipeline — 5 Million Concurrent Connections

Why WebSockets, not HTTP polling

An HTTP poll every 4 seconds from 5 million drivers means 1.25 million TCP connections opened and closed per second, each with a full TLS handshake. That is an order of magnitude more CPU overhead than maintaining persistent connections. WebSockets also eliminate the latency of connection setup per message: a location update becomes a single frame on an already-open socket.

Architecture of the gateway layer

  • Gateway nodes: Each handles 10,000–50,000 concurrent WebSocket connections. At 5 million connections you need ~100–500 gateway pods behind a load balancer.
  • Sticky sessions: All frames from driver ID X always route to the same gateway node. This keeps the routing state local and avoids cross-node fan-out on every message.
  • Heartbeat: Ping/pong every 30 seconds. If no pong, close connection and remove driver from Redis within 35 seconds to avoid stale location data.
  • Message format: Binary frames (MessagePack or Protobuf), not JSON text. Saves ~40% bandwidth at scale.

Matching Algorithm — The Full Dispatch Protocol

Uber does not simply assign the geographically closest driver. It assigns the driver likely to arrive soonest, weighted by rating and penalised for high-surge zones. The full protocol is a supply pool search → parallel ETA scoring → ranked offer wave dispatch. 


Rider taps "Request ride"
  │
  ▼
Step 1 — Supply Pool Search
  Redis GEOSEARCH around rider (H3 ring 1 → ring 5 if needed)
  Returns: [driver:d001, driver:d002, ... driver:d047]
  Target: 10–20 candidate drivers
  │
  ▼
Step 2 — Parallel ETA Computation (100 ms budget)
  For each candidate (all in parallel):
    Call ETA engine with (driver_pos, rider_pos)
    Returns: estimated arrival time in seconds
  Filter: discard ETA > 15 min
  Result: [(d001, 3m20s), (d004, 4m05s), (d009, 5m12s), ...]
  │
  ▼
Step 3 — Scoring
  score(driver) =
    0.5 × (1 - ETA / max_ETA_in_pool)   // lower ETA → higher score
  + 0.3 × (driver.rating / 5.0)          // higher rating → higher score
  + 0.2 × (1 - surge_multiplier / 10.0)  // lower surge → higher score
  │
  ▼
Step 4 — Wave Dispatch (Timeout Chain)
  Wave 1: Offer sent to top-1 driver      — 30 sec timeout
  Wave 2: Offer sent to top 2–3 drivers   — 20 sec timeout (if wave 1 no response)
  Wave 3: Offer sent to top 4–8 drivers   — 15 sec timeout
  Wave 4: Expand search radius, restart from Step 1
  │
  ▼
Step 5 — Allocation (Distributed Lock)
  First driver to accept:
    1. Acquire Redis lock: SET rider:{id}:lock {token} EX 10 NX
    2. Verify rider state is still "requesting" (not cancelled)
    3. INSERT INTO trips (...) ON CONFLICT DO NOTHING
    4. Release lock via Lua script (atomic check-and-delete)
    5. Publish "offer_expired" to all other candidate drivers

Full dispatch protocol — from rider tap to confirmed match

Distributed lock — preventing double-match

The race condition: two drivers press Accept within milliseconds of each other. Without a lock, both get a "trip created" confirmation and both drive to the pickup. This is the most important correctness constraint in the entire system. 


// Node.js — Redis lock acquire/release
const redis = require('ioredis');
const { v4: uuid } = require('uuid');

async function acquireLock(riderId, ttlSec = 10) {
  const token = uuid();
  // SET NX is atomic — only one caller wins
  const ok = await redis.set(
    `rider:${riderId}:lock`,
    token,
    'EX', ttlSec,
    'NX'             // only set if key does NOT exist
  );
  return ok === 'OK' ? token : null;
}

// Lua script: check token matches before deleting (atomic)
const RELEASE_SCRIPT = `
  if redis.call("GET", KEYS[1]) == ARGV[1] then
    return redis.call("DEL", KEYS[1])
  else
    return 0
  end
`;

async function releaseLock(riderId, token) {
  return redis.eval(RELEASE_SCRIPT, 1, `rider:${riderId}:lock`, token);
}

// Usage in match handler
async function confirmMatch(riderId, driverId) {
  const token = await acquireLock(riderId);
  if (!token) throw new Error('Rider already matched');

  try {
    const rider = await getRider(riderId);
    if (rider.status !== 'requesting') return; // already cancelled or matched

    await db.query(
      `INSERT INTO trips (rider_id, driver_id, status)
       VALUES ($1, $2, 'accepted')
       ON CONFLICT (rider_id) DO NOTHING`,
      [riderId, driverId]
    );
  } finally {
    await releaseLock(riderId, token);
  }
}

Redis SET NX + Lua release — the only reliable way to prevent double-match

Deep Dive 4

ETA Engine — Road Network + A* + Hybrid Traffic

The road network as a weighted directed graph

Every intersection is a node. Every road segment is a directed edge (one-way streets are single-direction edges). Each edge carries: distance in metres, speed limit, turn restrictions, road type (motorway / arterial / local). The graph for a single city has millions of nodes.

Dijkstra vs A*

Algorithmf(n)Search patternSpeed
Dijkstra g(n) — actual cost from start Expands uniformly in all directions O(E + V log V)
A* g(n) + h(n) — actual + heuristic Focuses toward the goal (straight-line estimate) 3–10× faster than Dijkstra on road graphs

A* works correctly as long as the heuristic h(n) is admissible — it never overestimates the true remaining cost. The great-circle (Haversine) distance divided by the fastest possible road speed is a perfect admissible heuristic for road routing.

Hybrid traffic model


// For each road segment edge:
estimated_speed =
  0.4 × real_time_speed[segment_id]         // Kafka stream, refreshed every 5 min
  + 0.6 × historical_avg_speed[segment_id]  // lookup by [hour_of_day][day_of_week]

// Edge travel time
edge_time_sec = edge_distance_m / (estimated_speed × 1000 / 3600)

// A* heuristic: great-circle distance / max possible speed
h(node) = haversine(node, destination) / MAX_SPEED_MPS

Traffic blending — 40% live data, 60% historical profile

Two-tier ETA computation

  • Fast ETA (30–50 ms): Run A* on a small subgraph (1 km radius), only to a depth of 100 nodes. Good enough for ranking the supply pool. Applied to all 20 candidates simultaneously in parallel goroutines / async workers.
  • Accurate ETA (200–500 ms): Full A* on the complete road network for the top 3 candidates. Used for the quoted arrival time shown to the rider.
  • Cache: Key = hash(from_h3_10, to_h3_10, hour_of_day), TTL 30 s. Cache hit rate ~65% for common origin/destination pairs.

Surge Pricing — Real-Time Streaming with Flink

The pipeline


Kafka: "driver-locations" topic
Kafka: "ride-requests"   topic
  │
  ▼
Flink Streaming Job (5-second tumbling window per H3 res-10 cell)
  │
  ├── Count online drivers in cell       → supply_count
  ├── Count open ride requests in cell   → demand_count
  └── Compute demand_ratio = demand_count / max(supply_count, 1)
  │
  ▼
Surge Multiplier Computation
  raw_surge = 1.0 + (demand_ratio - 1.0) × elasticity_factor
  surge     = clamp(raw_surge, min=1.0, max=10.0)

  // Smooth with EMA to prevent price jitter
  surge_smooth = 0.3 × surge + 0.7 × surge_smooth_prev
  │
  ▼
Redis SETEX "surge:{cell_id}" surge_smooth EX 60
  │
  ▼
Publish updated price to rider app via WebSocket broadcast
(all riders in that H3 cell receive the update)

Surge pricing pipeline — price updates lag demand by < 10 seconds

Elasticity factors by market

  • Dense metro (NYC, SF): 2.5 — riders have alternatives, price is a strong signal.
  • Suburban / rural: 1.2 — fewer drivers, lower price sensitivity.
  • Special events (concerts, airports): 3.0–5.0 — manually elevated ceiling.

Regulatory guardrails

  • Hard cap at 10× base fare in most jurisdictions.
  • After 15 consecutive minutes of surge, Uber switches to driver incentives (bonuses) rather than price escalation.
  • Rider sees the exact multiplier before confirming — no bait-and-switch.

Deep Dive 7

Fault Tolerance & Distributed System Gotchas

CAP theorem — which services are AP vs CP

DataCAP trade-offReasoning
Driver location AP (availability + partition tolerance) Stale by 4 s is fine; location will update; showing wrong position briefly is acceptable
Trip / payment CP (consistency + partition tolerance) Cannot charge rider twice or create two trips — ACID required
Surge price AP A stale price for 10 s is fine; correctness matters less than liveness
Match lock CP The double-match invariant must hold under any failure

Redis cluster failover

  • Each shard: 1 primary + 1 replica. Cluster spread across 3 availability zones.
  • Primary failure detected via gossip protocol in < 1 second.
  • Replica election requires majority quorum (> N/2 nodes agree).
  • Clients auto-reconnect to new primary; in-flight GEOADD commands retried.
  • Lock TTL (10 s) ensures any failed match attempt auto-releases before the next retry window.

Kafka exactly-once for location updates


// Consumer config
const consumer = kafka.consumer({
  groupId: 'location-ingest',
  enableAutoCommit: false,          // manual commit after successful write
  isolationLevel: 'read_committed'  // only consume committed messages
});

await consumer.run({
  eachBatch: async ({ batch, commitOffsetsIfNecessary }) => {
    for (const msg of batch.messages) {
      const update = JSON.parse(msg.value);
      await redis.geoadd('drivers:active', update.lng, update.lat, `driver:${update.id}`);
      await cassandra.execute(INSERT_STMT, [update.id, update.day, update.ts, ...]);
    }
    // Commit only after both writes succeed — idempotent on replay
    await commitOffsetsIfNecessary();
  }
});

Manual offset commit — if the consumer crashes mid-batch, it replays from last committed offset. GEOADD and Cassandra inserts are idempotent.

Interview Prep

What Interviewers Actually Look For — Company by Company

The 45-minute structure

  • 5 min — Clarify requirements: Confirm scale numbers (100 M riders, 5 M drivers, 10 M trips/day). Ask: "Should I prioritise matching latency or cost efficiency?"
  • 10 min — HLD: Draw the five services, data stores, and the two async paths (Kafka → Redis for hot; Kafka → Cassandra for cold).
  • 20 min — Deep dives: Pick two: geospatial indexing (H3 vs Geohash trade-off) and the matching dispatch protocol (supply pool + ETA + wave offers + distributed lock).
  • 8 min — Scale & bottlenecks: Walk through 1.25 M writes/sec → Redis sharding → where the bottleneck moves next.
  • 2 min — Graceful follow-ups: Show intellectual humility: "Good catch — I assumed TCP connections would be the limit. Switching to UDP for location would change the gateway design like this…"

Google

  • ✓ Trade-offs by the numbers: "Why Redis over PostGIS? Redis GEOADD is O(log N) for writes; PostGIS would need a GiST index rebuild on every update — untenable at 1.25 M writes/sec."
  • ✓ Graph algorithms: A* heuristic admissibility — can you prove the great-circle heuristic never over-estimates?
  • ✓ Distributed consensus: walk through the Lua lock release script without being prompted.
  • ✗ Avoid: treating surge pricing as an afterthought. Pricing is core revenue; Google interviewers will probe it.

Meta

  • ✓ Observability: "How do you monitor location update end-to-end latency? Which dashboard shows you the first sign of a Redis slowdown?"
  • ✓ Load testing: "How do you validate the system at 5 M concurrent WebSocket connections before going live?"
  • ✓ ML integration: "How would you use Cassandra's historical traces to improve the A* speed profiles?"
  • ✗ Avoid: hand-waving on QPS. Every number you state should be derivable — show the arithmetic.

Microsoft

  • ✓ Reliability SLA: "What is the MTTR if the Redis primary fails? Walk through the gossip election + client reconnect timeline."
  • ✓ API backward compatibility: "How do you version the WebSocket message schema when you add new fields?"
  • ✓ Security: "How do you authenticate driver WebSocket connections? Bearer JWT + server-side validation on every frame."
  • ✗ Avoid: ignoring compliance. Microsoft clients care about data residency for location data.

Salesforce

  • ✓ CAP theorem application: "Location is AP; trips are CP. Explain why and what breaks if you flip it."
  • ✓ Testing strategy: "How do you test that the double-match prevention actually works under concurrent load? Chaos engineering — kill the Redis primary during a high-volume wave dispatch."
  • ✓ Audit / governance: "Every trip mutation goes through the PostgreSQL write replica with an append-only audit log. Explain why you can't use Cassandra for this."
  • ✗ Avoid: missing the multi-tenancy angle. Salesforce will ask how you'd serve multiple city markets from one deployment.

High-signal phrases that separate senior answers

  • "The p99 target of 200 ms means we cannot afford sequential ETA computation — all 10 candidate drivers must be evaluated in parallel."
  • "I'd rather have a slightly stale location update than a dropped frame — location is AP, so Redis replication lag is acceptable."
  • "The Lua lock release is non-negotiable. A plain GET + DEL sequence has a TOCTOU race condition."
  • "H3 ring expansion is O(1) per ring because the neighbour relationship is encoded in the cell index — it is not a spatial query."
  • "We detect the dead driver connection in < 35 seconds (30-second ping + 5-second grace). That means a rider could wait 35 seconds before the system tries the next driver — I would reduce the heartbeat interval to 15 seconds at the cost of gateway CPU."

Red flags that tank interviews

  • ✗ "We'll just use Geohash" — without mentioning boundary discontinuity.
  • ✗ "Redis handles everything" — without discussing memory cost, sharding, or TTL expiry strategy.
  • ✗ "A lock prevents double-match" — without explaining what happens when the lock holder crashes before releasing.
  • ✗ "We scale horizontally" — without identifying what the next bottleneck is after adding nodes.
  • ✗ Skipping failure scenarios — every component should have a "what if this dies" answer.

Key Takeaway

Uber's matching speed is the product of three compounding optimisations: H3 hexagonal indexing that reduces 5 million global drivers to 50 local candidates in one lookup; WebSocket push connections that eliminate TCP handshake overhead at 1.25 million updates per second; and parallel A* ETA computation that scores every candidate simultaneously rather than sequentially. None of these alone is enough — the system's latency budget is held by the slowest link, so all three must work together.

In the interview, the design is secondary to the reasoning. What earns the hire decision is surfacing the trade-offs unprompted: why AP for location but CP for payments, why Lua on the lock release, why a wave dispatch rather than sending offers to all drivers simultaneously. Show you have thought past the happy path.
