CAP Theorem

In a distributed system, you can have at most two of three: Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance. Not as a design choice — as a mathematical proof. Understanding what CP and AP systems actually mean, and the PACELC extension that makes the trade-off more nuanced in practice.

The CAP theorem is one of the most cited and most misunderstood concepts in distributed systems. "You can't have all three" is correct but incomplete. The nuance: partition tolerance is not optional — network partitions happen in any distributed system. So the real choice is always CP or AP: when a partition occurs, do you maintain consistency (refuse requests that might be stale) or availability (serve requests that might be stale)?

The correct framing: CA (no partition tolerance) only applies to a single-node system — which is not distributed. Every distributed system must tolerate partitions. Therefore the real choice is: CP (sacrifice availability during a partition) or AP (sacrifice consistency during a partition). Choose based on which failure mode hurts your business more.

The Trade-off

CP vs AP — behaviour during a network partition

What a network partition looks like Node A ─────────┐ ┌───── Node B │ │ │ NETWORK │ │ SPLIT │ └── ✗ ✗ ──┘ A and B can't communicate. Write arrives at A. Does B serve reads? CP choice: Consistency over Availability During partition: Node B refuses reads/writes (or returns error) Reason: it cannot confirm it has the latest data from Node A. Result: All reads return consistent (current) data but service is unavailable on partitioned nodes Examples: etcd, ZooKeeper, HBase, single-leader PostgreSQL (sync replication) When to use: leader election, distributed locks, financial ledgers. AP choice: Availability over Consistency During partition: Node B continues serving reads/writes Reason: availability is more important than perfect consistency. Result: Service stays available everywhere but reads from B may return stale data until partition heals Examples: Cassandra, DynamoDB (default), CouchDB, DNS When to use: user activity feeds, shopping carts, product catalogs, DNS. PACELC extension: the partition-free trade-off During partition (P): choose Availability (A) vs Consistency (C) Else (E, no partition): choose Latency (L) vs Consistency (C) Even without a partition, replication has latency. Strong consistency requires waiting for replicas → higher latency. Eventual consistency doesn't wait → lower latency. Example: DynamoDB is AP/EL — AP during partitions, low-latency eventual reads normally. Example: Spanner is CP/EC — CP during partitions, consistent but higher latency normally.

Most applications don't need to choose. For a typical application with a single-region PostgreSQL primary and read replicas: partitions between primary and replicas are rare and brief. Your data's availability and consistency requirements are probably met without thinking about CAP at all. CAP becomes a real design constraint when you're building globally distributed systems with nodes in multiple regions that must continue operating independently during a region outage — and you need to decide what those nodes do during the split.

The shopping cart example — Amazon's famous AP choice

Amazon deliberately chose AP for shopping carts. The reasoning: if a network partition occurs, it is better to allow a user to add items to their cart (available, possibly stale) than to refuse any cart interaction (consistent, unavailable). At worst, the cart might have a duplicate item that gets resolved when the partition heals. The business cost of "cart showed an extra item temporarily" is far lower than "user could not add to cart at all." This is a deliberate, reasoned AP choice — not an accident.

Key Takeaway

Every distributed system must tolerate partitions — so the real CAP choice is CP or AP. CP systems (etcd, PostgreSQL sync replication) refuse requests that can't be guaranteed consistent during a partition — they prioritise correctness over availability. AP systems (Cassandra, DynamoDB default) continue serving during a partition, accepting that some reads may be stale — they prioritise availability over consistency. Choose CP for financial ledgers, distributed locks, and leader election. Choose AP for user feeds, shopping carts, product catalogs, and DNS — where brief inconsistency is tolerable but downtime is not. PACELC extends CAP to also capture the latency-consistency trade-off in the partition-free case.
