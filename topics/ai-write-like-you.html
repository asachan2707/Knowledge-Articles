The Best Way to Make AI Write Like You

Most AI-generated writing sounds the same — corporate, cautious, and obviously not you. This guide covers the proven techniques to capture your personal voice in a system prompt and make any LLM write in your register consistently.

FocusVoice cloning via prompting, style guides, few-shot examples
Works withClaude, ChatGPT, Gemini — model-agnostic
LevelBeginner to intermediate

AI will not write like you by default. It writes like the average of the internet — which is why everything it produces has the same sanitised tone. The fix is not a cleverer prompt. It is a system: specific examples of your writing, explicit style rules, and negative examples of what you do not sound like.

Core idea: Style is a distribution. To shift the model's output into your register, you need to feed it enough of your own writing that it learns the shape of your voice — then lock that shape in with explicit rules.

Why AI writing sounds generic

LLMs are trained to predict what text comes next across billions of documents. The most probable continuation of any prompt is the most average text ever written on that topic. That is not your voice. Your voice is the deviation from average — your sentence rhythm, your vocabulary choices, your structural signatures. Those only emerge if you explicitly condition the model toward them.

The core technique

Few-shot examples are your highest-leverage tool

The single most effective technique is providing real samples of your own writing tagged as style references. Fewer than five examples produces inconsistent results. Twelve or more is the practical floor for reliable style consistency. The examples should match the distribution of your actual writing — if 80% of what you write is casual, do not weight the examples toward formal prose. 

<style_sample>
[Paste 200–500 words of your actual writing here]
</style_sample>

Using the voice and style demonstrated above, write [task].

Build a style decomposition

Vague style descriptions ("write casually") do not work reliably. Decompose your voice into specific, measurable attributes:

  • Sentence length pattern — short and punchy, long and flowing, or mixed?
  • Punctuation signatures — em-dashes, parenthetical asides, semicolons?
  • Vocabulary register — technical, plain, or domain-specific slang?
  • Contraction usage — always, never, or contextual?
  • Opening patterns — anecdote, question, bold claim?
  • Paragraph pacing — one idea per paragraph or richer multi-idea blocks?

Add negative examples

Show the model what you explicitly do not sound like. This is underused but highly effective. Example: 

DO NOT write: "Leverage synergistic frameworks to optimise outcomes."
DO write: "Use tools that actually work."

DO NOT write: "It is important to note that..."
DO write: "Here's the thing —"

Role assignment

Open your system prompt with an identity block. "You are [your name], a writer who…" followed by 3–5 defining behavioural traits. For instruction-following models, explicit sentence-level rules outperform vague personality descriptions.

Advanced techniques

Prefilling and scratchpad analysis

Prefilling

Start the model's response with your own first sentence. Claude and GPT-4 will continue from your exact register when you supply the opening phrase. This is the fastest way to lock in tone on short-form pieces.

Style analysis scratchpad

Add a hidden reasoning step where the model analyses your sample text before generating output: 

First, in a <scratchpad>, analyse the style of my sample:
- Average sentence length
- Top 3 vocabulary patterns
- Structural signature (how paragraphs open and close)
- Tone on a formal-casual spectrum

Then write [task] matching those exact parameters.

This forces the model to explicitly extract your style before generating, rather than approximating it from a general impression. It improves consistency noticeably on longer pieces.

The system prompt structure

The three-block template

Structure your reusable style system prompt in three blocks: 

# Identity
You are [name], a [role] who writes about [topics].
Your writing is [3 trait adjectives].

# Style rules
- Sentences average [short / medium / long]
- Use contractions always / never
- Prefer [specific punctuation patterns]
- Never start a sentence with "Additionally" or "Furthermore"
- [Your specific rules]

# Negative examples
AVOID: [Example of writing you hate]
USE: [Example of writing you love]

# Writing samples
<sample_1>[Your actual writing]</sample_1>
<sample_2>[Your actual writing]</sample_2>

Place this in the system prompt position (not the user turn) — it survives context compaction better there and applies to every message in the session.

Fine-tuning vs prompting

When to go beyond prompting

Sophisticated prompting covers 90% of personal voice use cases. Fine-tuning on 50–100 examples of your writing produces a permanently style-locked model — but requires dataset curation and cost, and the result is not easily updated as your style evolves. For most individuals, a well-crafted system prompt beats fine-tuning in both speed and flexibility.

Tools like Jasper, Copy.ai, and Anyword offer brand-voice features that auto-extract style parameters from your uploaded writing. Useful starting points, but they lack the precision of a hand-crafted style guide.

Common questions

FAQ

  • How many writing samples is enough?

    12 or more samples of 200–500 words each is the practical floor. Fewer produces inconsistent results. Vary the samples across topics and formats you actually write in.

  • Does this work if my style is still evolving?

    Yes — just update your samples periodically. The system prompt is not permanent. Treat it like any document you maintain: review quarterly and replace samples that no longer represent you.

  • Will the model eventually drift back to generic?

    In very long conversations, yes. Re-anchor by repeating the identity block or starting a fresh session with your system prompt already loaded. Short sessions with a persistent system prompt maintain style reliably.

Key Takeaway

AI writes like you when you give it enough of you to work from. Collect 12+ real writing samples, decompose your voice into explicit rules, add negative examples of what you hate, and structure it all in a persistent system prompt. Specificity beats cleverness — the more precise the instructions, the fewer corrections you need.
