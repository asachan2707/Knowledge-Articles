The #1 Claude Skill That Helps Me Make Better Decisions

Not a prompt template — a mental model shift. Stop asking Claude "what should I do?" and start asking it to surface what you are rationalising away. Here is the technique stack that makes Claude genuinely useful for decisions, not just validating ones you already made.

FocusStructured decision prompting, devil's advocate, pre-mortem
Works withClaude — benefits from its honesty training

Most people use Claude for decisions the wrong way. They describe their situation, their inclination, and then ask for confirmation. Claude, trained to be helpful, often confirms. The skill is making Claude disagree with you — and specifically disagree with the parts you have not examined.

Core idea: Claude's value in decisions comes from its combination of breadth (it has processed millions of decision contexts) and honesty training (it will flag what you are rationalising, if you ask it to). The question is whether you ask it to.

Why Claude is different for this

Claude's training explicitly targets intellectual honesty — it is designed to disagree with users when warranted, acknowledge its own uncertainty rather than manufacturing false confidence, and surface uncomfortable trade-offs rather than smoothing them over. It will tell you your plan has a flaw if you create the conditions for it to do so. Most users do not create those conditions.

The #1 skill

Structured decision prompting

The most reliable technique is the structured decision prompt that forces Claude to argue all sides before you get a recommendation: 

I'm deciding between [option A] and [option B].
Context: [situation in 2-3 sentences].
Constraint: [budget / time / other limits].

Please:
1. List the 3 strongest arguments FOR each option
2. List the 3 strongest arguments AGAINST each option
3. Identify 2-3 things I probably haven't considered
4. What would you ask me before giving a recommendation?
5. Your honest lean, with the biggest uncertainty flagged

Step 3 is where the value is. Claude has broad exposure to how decisions like yours play out. It will name failure modes and second-order consequences you genuinely have not thought of — if the prompt explicitly invites it to.

Five supporting techniques

A full decision toolkit

1. Pre-mortem analysis

I'm planning to [decision]. Assume 12 months from now
this went badly. Walk me through the 3 most likely failure
modes and the early warning signs I should watch for.

Pre-mortems force you to think concretely about failure before you are committed. Claude is particularly good at this because it can draw on patterns from similar decisions across many domains you have not encountered.

2. Devil's advocate — unsoftened

I've decided to [X]. Take the strongest possible position
AGAINST this decision. Do not soften it or balance it with
positives. I want the sharpest version of the case against.

Most AI tools soften pushback by default. This prompt explicitly removes the softening. The result is uncomfortable and useful.

3. Criteria-first evaluation

Before I tell you my choice, help me define what criteria
should matter for this type of decision: [context].
List 5-8 criteria and weight them 1-10 by importance.

Define your decision criteria before you reveal your inclination. This prevents the common pattern of unconsciously selecting criteria that justify your existing preference.

4. Steelman the opposing view

I'm leaning toward [A] over [B]. Steelman [B] — build the
strongest possible version of the case for it, even including
arguments I haven't raised.

A steelman is the opposite of a strawman: you build the strongest version of the opposing position. If you cannot steelman the option you rejected, you probably do not understand the trade-off fully.

5. The interview technique

I'm trying to decide [X]. Interview me about this decision.
Ask me about things I may not have considered. Keep asking
until we've covered all angles, then give me a decision brief.

This inverts the normal conversation pattern. Claude asks; you answer. The questions it asks will surface gaps you did not know existed.

What not to do

The pattern that wastes Claude's decision-making potential

The single most common mistake: describing your situation and your inclination in the same message, then asking "what do you think?" Claude interprets this as a request for validation, not challenge. It will find merits in your inclination. The fix is separating the two — give context without your lean, ask for analysis, then reveal your preference and ask Claude to argue against it.

Key Takeaway

The skill is not a specific prompt — it is the habit of asking Claude to disagree with you rigorously before you decide. Use the structured decision prompt for the core framing, follow it with a pre-mortem and a devil's advocate pass, and define your criteria before you reveal your preference. Claude's honesty training means it will surface what you are rationalising away — but only if your prompt creates the space for it to do so.
