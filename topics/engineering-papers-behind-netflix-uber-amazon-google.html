Story · "Always writable"

The author opens with Dynamo — Amazon's 2007 paper that introduced the idea of sacrificing strong consistency for availability. The slogan was "always writable": even when nodes disagree, accept the write and resolve conflicts later.

That single trade-off, formalized in one paper, became the design lineage behind DynamoDB, Cassandra, Riak, and a generation of NoSQL systems.

Detailed breakdown

Key insights and why they matter

1) Dynamo (2007) — availability over consistency

Consistent hashing, vector clocks, quorum reads/writes, eventual consistency. Foundational to DynamoDB and Cassandra.

2) MapReduce (2004) — batch computation at scale

The map/shuffle/reduce model that powered the first generation of big-data systems and inspired Hadoop, Spark, and modern data platforms.

3) GFS / Bigtable (2003, 2006) — Google's storage substrate

How to build planet-scale file systems and wide-column stores on commodity hardware with replication and failure assumptions baked in.

4) Spanner (2012) — strong consistency at global scale

TrueTime, external consistency, and Paxos-based replication. Showed that the CAP-style "you can't have it all" was more nuanced than people assumed.

5) Raft (2014) — consensus you can actually understand

A consensus algorithm explicitly designed for understandability vs Paxos. Now powers etcd, Consul, CockroachDB, and many internal systems.

6) Kafka (2011) — the log as a primitive

The append-only log as a system abstraction. Reshaped how teams think about event sourcing, change data capture, and async architectures.

7) CAP theorem and its successors

The constraint that gets oversimplified. Read Brewer's original notes plus the "PACELC" extension to see what the trade-off really looks like in practice.

Practical learning

How to apply this in real work

  • Read one paper per week. Write a 5-bullet summary of the trade-off it formalized.
  • For each paper, name one production system you have used that inherits its design.
  • Use paper vocabulary in your next architecture review (quorum, eventual consistency, log compaction, etc.).
  • Build a personal index by theme: storage, consensus, scheduling, messaging.
Common questions

FAQ

  • Do I need to read these cover to cover?

    No. Start with abstract + intro + one diagram. Re-read the key mechanism only if your work touches it.

  • Are these still relevant in 2026?

    Yes. The hardware is faster, the trade-offs are the same. Strong consistency vs availability, batch vs stream, sync vs async — all unchanged.

  • Where do I start?

    Dynamo + MapReduce + Raft. They cover three of the most-reused trade-off families.
